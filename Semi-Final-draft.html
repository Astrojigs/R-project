Introduction

The data files we worked with contain real life information from the annual bicycle race, The Tour de France. Riders from all over the world participate in the event. The Tour de France is a multiple stage race which takes place over several days. Usually, the race is held in France but occasionally a stage or indeed the entire event is held in another country. The Tour de France began in 1903, and the initial years consisted mostly of French riders or their close neighbours. Sports science, bicycle technology and indeed pharmaceuticals have evolved over the years. Also testing and standardization have been introduced. The data we were presented with consisted of three files, two of which focus on stage data and one on winners data. There are many analysis opportunities with this data, for the purpose of this project we have chosen to focus on how athletes performance is affected by personal attributes such as physicality, age, and country of birth, the data of which is obtained from the tdf_winners data file. Our report explores the following questions:

  1. Does an athletes country of birth affect their results?

We check if the athletes country of birth has any effect on the results of the competition. Will traits such as adapting to atmospheric pressure and the prevailing climate in Western Europe affect performance. Do you have an advantage over other riders if you are from the country the race is held in?

  1. Does an athletes BMI influence their speed?

We test how fitness affects competition results. Is there a relationship between health and winning the trophy? We look for a relationship between the body mass index (BMI) and the average velocity for each athlete to answer this question.

  1. Is there an age group that performs best?

We will explore the various age groups of the winners. We create age groups and allocate an appropriate age group to each athlete. We look for a relationship between the age and the average velocity for each athlete to find a concise answer to this question. The data contains several personal specifications related to our research such as height and weight. These are used to create a new variable body mass index (BMI), a very significant pointer in our case study. The data also contains the distance traveled in KM across the entire race and the overall time taken by the winner to complete the race. We use this information to calculate the average velocity for each competitor.

Conclusions

  1. Does an athletes country of birth affect their results?

At first look we would say yes, athletes from France or neighbouring countries have a distinct advantage over their competitors. 80% of the winners are from France, Belgium, Spain, and Italy. Coming from a country with a similar terrain and weather as the country of the competition means theres a higher probability of winning the competition. However, we cannot overlook the fact that we do not have genetic specifications and we have no data on where athletes live while preparing for the race. The assumption we make is the data is skewed as over the years most competitors came from France or neighbouring countries. Being from France does not make you a better athlete, there are just more French athletes competing.

  1. Does an athletes BMI influence their speed?

Yes, the athletes BMI does influence speed. From the graph we can clearly see the relationship between body mass index and average velocity is a linear relationship with a negative slope. Considering the BMI outliers (16 & 29) we are still confident concluding that athletes with a lower BMI are faster.

  1. Is there an age group that performs best?

This is interesting as there is a small range of winners ages 19-36. The median age of a winner is 28. This coincides with our findings relating to speed. We created three age categories 18-24, 25-30 and 30-40. We can see from the graph that the 25-30 category raced with a higher average speed than the other groups. So, some experience is good, but the athletes will most likely hit their speed peak in their late 20s.
